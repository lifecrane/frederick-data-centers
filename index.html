Adamstown East & the Data Center Mess

A developer-driven rezoning, probably a contaminated brownfield at old plant, a council vote two days before Christmas, an ethics fine, and an annexation loophole that nobody closed. Here's what happened and why it matters.

📍 Adamstown, MD 📅 Updated June 2026 🔗 Share this page
The short version

What you need to know

  • The data centers were approved in December 2021 — four years before the CDI overlay existed. The overlay didn't enable them. It was created afterward to contain the next wave.
  • There was no legal use category for "data center" in Frederick County or the City of Frederick before 2025. Developers pushed ad-hoc amendments into a legal vacuum.
  • The County Council voted 5-2 on December 23, 2025 to approve a 2,615-acre CDI overlay — nearly double the Planning Commission's recommendation — with no public comment on the amended ordinance.
  • The site is a contaminated brownfield — the former Alcoa/Eastalco aluminum smelting plant. Air quality, water, and hazmat permits remain unconfirmed publicly.
  • A 100 MW data center consumes ~528,000 gallons of water per day. The Adamstown campus is projected at 300–500 MW. Frederick's water infrastructure was never planned for this.
  • Loudoun County is the warning nobody heeded. Dominion Energy cannot meet Loudoun's data center power demand. Residential rates are going up 14%. Grid violations were anticipated in 2025. Frederick is importing the same model with none of the runway.
  • The City of Frederick still has no legal data center use category. A proposed text amendment filed by Trammell Crow's attorney in December 2025 remains pending — and a city council member was fined for ethics violations tied to her role in drafting an identical amendment.
  • The annexation loophole is real. A developer can seek annexation into the City of Frederick to escape the county's CDI-OZ boundary entirely — and the city and county have no unified framework to stop it.
  • A referendum petition launched in January 2026 — and was struck down in court in June 2026. A Circuit Court judge ruled the petition's reproduction of the ordinance was inadequate and that zoning ordinances aren't subject to referendum under the county charter. The referendum committee has appealed.

Part 1 — Frederick County

The CDI Overlay Story

How a 2021 approval became a 2025 political firestorm — and what the Loudoun County precedent tells us about where Frederick is headed.

01 — Base zoning

What Adamstown East was before

The Eastalco site north of Adamstown is the former Alcoa/Eastalco aluminum smelting plant. Its base zoning carries Agricultural and Limited Industrial designations. A zoning overlay adds permitted uses on top — it doesn't replace base zoning rights.

There was no legal category for "data center" in Frederick County before 2025. The county had to create one from scratch — while projects were already under construction.

02 — The buried fact

Approvals came 4 years before the overlay

The data centers under construction north of Adamstown received their preliminary plan and Adequate Public Facilities Ordinance (APFO) approvals in December 2021 — years before any CDI framework existed. The overlay did not authorize them. It was created afterward to contain the next wave.

04 — What was bypassed

The controversy

No public comment on the amended map

The Council amended the Planning Commission's recommendation — nearly doubling acreage — and voted two days before Christmas with no public comment opportunity on the amended ordinance.

Overwhelming public opposition ignored

The Sugarloaf Alliance and other groups documented overwhelming opposition. 5 of 7 Council members voted for the larger map anyway.

Contaminated brownfield — no disclosed monitoring

The Eastalco/Alcoa site has legacy contamination from aluminum smelting. No federal or state agency proactively disclosed environmental monitoring data. MDE contacted — jurisdiction response: unclear.

500-foot residential setback

The bill allows data centers adjacent to residential areas with only a 500-foot property line setback. Council member McKay called this "lousy public policy."

Possible illegal contract zoning

The 5:1 agricultural offset provision (5 preserved acres per new acre converted) raised concerns it could constitute illegal contract zoning — trading a development right for an environmental concession beyond the county's legal authority.

05 — The Loudoun warning

What "the next Northern Virginia" actually looks like

⚠️ Frederick residents said they didn't want to be Loudoun County. Here's what Loudoun County became.

Dominion Energy cannot meet the energy demands of data centers already approved in Loudoun. The utility proposed new 500 kV and 230 kV transmission lines through residential and commercial areas — and even those fall short. More lines will be needed.

Dominion proposed a 14% residential rate increase in 2026 citing data center growth. Using 2023 as a base, Loudoun could require 20–30 GW of power by 2028 due to AI demands alone — a load PJM and Dominion are unlikely to meet.

On March 18, 2025, Loudoun's Board of Supervisors voted 7-2 to end all "by right" data center development. They got there after 200 approved data centers and a grid in crisis. Frederick is being offered the same model at the start, not the end.

06 — Root causes

Why this became a mess — systemic problems

01

No existing zoning category

No legal use classification for data centers existed. Developers pushed ad-hoc amendments into a legal vacuum rather than a planned framework.

02

Reactive policymaking

The county only began regulating data centers after hyperscale projects were already approved in December 2021. The overlay was built to contain a problem already in motion.

03

Environmental contamination on a brownfield

Legacy Alcoa contamination on the Eastalco site. Construction-related earthmoving and runoff risks at a brownfield. No proactive agency disclosure of monitoring data.

04

Community backlash drove rushed policy

Residents demanded limits to avoid "the next Northern Virginia." This pushed officials into restrictive decisions made under political pressure rather than deliberate planning.

05

Over-concentration in one community

All data center development limited to less than 1% of county land in a single location — Adamstown — concentrating all political pressure, land-use conflict, and infrastructure strain in one neighborhood.

06

Infrastructure mismatch

Hyperscale data centers need massive power, water, and fiber. Frederick's infrastructure was never planned for this. Nobody has publicly answered who pays for upgrades.

07

Conflicting city vs. county frameworks

The county created a CDI-OZ. The city had to separately create an LMC amendment. Two parallel regulatory tracks, inconsistent rules, and an annexation gap between them. See the City Story for details.

08

Developer-driven rezoning requests

Property owners submitted their own inclusion/exclusion requests for the overlay map. The Noffsinger family — who relocated livestock, horses, and family members based on expected approval — illustrates the human cost of this uncertainty.

Part 2 — City of Frederick

The City Story

A legal gap that went unfixed for three years. An ethics-fined council member. A national developer with a backup plan. And an annexation loophole the county and city have no unified framework to close.

01 — The 2022 legal gap

Data centers were already unpermitted in 2022 — nothing was done

In 2022 a city planning official determined that data centers did not fit any existing use category in city code — and formally recommended a text amendment to fix it. No amendment was passed. The City Council did nothing.

The result: for three years, the City of Frederick operated with no legal framework for data centers whatsoever — while the county's Eastalco campus was already under construction a few miles away, and developers were quietly assembling land inside city limits.

The 2022 ruling

City planning staff concluded data centers were "establishments primarily engaged in providing infrastructure for hosting or data processing services" — a category that didn't match any permitted use. The staff memo recommended a text amendment. The Council tabled it. Three years passed.

02 — Katie Nash

The ethics violation at the center of it

⚠️ Ethics fine — May 2026 — $1,000 — second violation in four years

City Council member Katie Nash, who served as Council President for most of 2025, had been working on a draft text amendment to define data centers as a permitted use in city code — the fix the 2022 staff memo had recommended.

Nash withdrew her draft amendment. In December 2025, national developer Trammell Crow filed a text amendment through attorney Bruce Dean that was described as identical to Nash's August submission.

The problem: Nash was simultaneously working as a paid lobbyist for Vistra Corp., a national energy company — while sitting on the City Council and shaping data center policy. Energy generation and data center development are deeply connected industries; data centers are among the largest electricity consumers in the country.

Nash's defense: "It's not the same industry. It never occurred to me there's a conflict." The Frederick City Ethics Commission disagreed and issued a $1,000 fine on May 11, 2026 — her second ethics violation, following a 2022 ruling for using her position to lobby on behalf of a firefighters union client.

→ Full disciplinary record and accountability tracker in Legal Challenges

03 — Trammell Crow

Gas House Pike and the 231-acre play

National developer Trammell Crow, through land use attorney Bruce Dean, filed a text amendment to the city's Land Management Code on December 11, 2025 — the same week the County Council was voting on the CDI-OZ map.

What the proposed LMC amendment defines

The amendment would create "Critical Digital Infrastructure" and "Critical Digital Infrastructure Substations" as legal use categories in city code for the first time. It would limit data centers to parcels of 75 acres or larger in M1 (light industrial) and M2 (heavy industrial) zones only — reducing eligible locations within city limits to four known parcels.

The two target sites

Trammell Crow is targeting a 125-acre site at 8908 Gas House Pike and a 106-acre site at 6900 Linganore Road (annexed in 2023, previously the Mercer family farm, now sold to Trammell Crow). Both are near the Monocacy River. Total: 231 acres.

The warehouse backup plan

Trammell Crow already had a city-approved warehouse plan for the Gas House Pike site. In March 2026, they received a one-year extension on that warehouse approval — keeping their options open while the data center text amendment is pending. If the LMC amendment fails, they build a warehouse. If it passes, they build a data center.

04 — The loophole

The annexation risk nobody closed

This is the structural danger that makes the city and county stories inseparable.

How the loophole works

The county's CDI-OZ restricts data centers to a 2,615-acre boundary around Adamstown. But a developer can seek annexation of a parcel into City of Frederick limits — and then pursue approval under city code instead of county zoning. The city and county are developing their rules on separate, inconsistent tracks with no unified framework. The gap between them is the loophole.

The Linganore Road precedent

The 106-acre Linganore Road parcel was annexed into the City of Frederick in 2023 — before the CDI-OZ existed. It is now a candidate for a data center under the proposed city LMC amendment, on land that was never part of any county public debate about the overlay zone.

No unified framework exists

The county created the CDI-OZ under county zoning. The city is separately considering an LMC text amendment under city code. There is no intergovernmental agreement, no shared boundary protocol, and no mechanism to prevent future annexations from bypassing the county's restrictions entirely.

Bottom line

The county spent most of 2025 in a contentious public process to define where data centers can go. A motivated developer can sidestep the entire result by annexing a parcel into the city and pushing through a city-level approval instead. Until the city passes its own restrictions — or the city and county align their frameworks — the CDI-OZ boundary has a hole in it.

Part 3 — Legal Challenges

Accountability in Progress

The referendum's day in court, the Board of Elections' response, and the disciplinary actions tied to this story so far. This page will be updated as new filings, rulings, and appeals develop.

01 — The court rulings

What Happened in the Courts

The "Bonifant" Ruling (Circuit Court)

In mid-June, Frederick County Circuit Court Judge James A. Bonifant ruled in favor of the developers. He stated that under the Frederick County Charter, a zoning map change is not technically a "law," so it isn't eligible for a public referendum. He also ruled that the petition itself was legally flawed because it didn't include the full text of the ordinance or clear colorized zoning maps.

The Maryland Supreme Court Order

The referendum committee appealed directly to the state's highest court. On June 30, 2026, the Maryland Supreme Court issued a brief per curiam order completely upholding Judge Bonifant's decision. Because of this, the referendum will not be on the November ballot. The court noted they will release a full written opinion explaining their reasoning later.

A Note of Recognition

Despite the court's final ruling, the referendum committee and the hundreds of volunteers who mobilized across Frederick County deserve immense respect. In just a few months, they successfully organized, engaged the community, and certified over 20,000 signatures. While the ballot measure was ultimately blocked on procedural grounds, their dedication to civic participation and demanding a voice in local land-use decisions set a historic standard for grassroots advocacy in Maryland.

02 — The appeal

The referendum committee appeals

The Frederick County Data Center Referendum Committee filed an appeal of Bonifant's ruling. Elizabeth Bauer, the committee's lead organizer, shared a copy of the appeal publicly the night of the ruling. Because Maryland's deadline for submitting ballot language to the State Board of Elections is July 1, 2026, the appeal is on a compressed timeline.

Appellate filings, briefing schedule, and outcome will be added here as they become public.

03 — Board of Elections response

Emergency meeting — June 19, 2026

The Frederick County Board of Elections held an emergency open session on June 19, 2026 to discuss the ruling. The Board declined to take further action in response to the ruling.

Why this matters

The Board had certified the referendum petition's format as sufficient in April 2026 — before the committee collected any signatures. More than 20,000 residents relied on that certification when signing. The Court's ruling effectively overrode that certification after the fact, and the Board's decision not to pursue its own appeal leaves that tension unresolved.
